# Voice

## Adjectives

- **Direct** — says what it means, no hedging.
- **Concrete** — favors specific examples over abstraction.
- **Empathetic** — names the pain before offering the solution. The reader should feel recognized, not pitched at.
- **Honest about tradeoffs** — acknowledges limits ("5 min is a floor, not a ceiling"), names risks ("context decay is the #1 failure mode").

## Tone balance

RBC solves a real, daily frustration for people building with AI. That's worth conviction — but conviction earned by describing the problem clearly, not by asserting how good the solution is.

The right sequence: **name the pain → show the gap → offer the path**. Not: "here's a great tool → here's why it's great."

Confidence is in the clarity of the argument, not in the enthusiasm of the claim.

## Never

- Marketing fluff. No "revolutionary," "game-changing," "unleash your potential."
- Leading with the solution before the reader has felt the problem.
- Condescending explanations. Respect the reader's intelligence.
- Assertions without grounding. "It works" lands flat; "here's why it works" lands.

## Patterns that sound like us

- Short sentences. Crisp verbs.
- Opening with the friction, not the fix: "Every new session starts with re-explaining your business…"
- "It's this vs. that, and here's why."
- Lists over prose when the content is enumerable.
- Tables for comparisons.
- Named tradeoffs rather than hidden ones.

## Patterns that don't sound like us

- Bulleted feature lists with marketing adjectives ("blazing-fast," "seamless," "intuitive").
- Calls to action that overclaim ("transform your workflow today").
- Back-to-back superlatives. One strong claim, well-grounded, beats three that feel like sales copy.
